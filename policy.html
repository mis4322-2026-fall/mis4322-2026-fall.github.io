AI Usage Policy

This course treats AI as a real-world productivity tool, not as a shortcut around learning.

1) Policy Philosophy

  • AI use is allowed and expected in many course activities.
  • Students are responsible for understanding what they submit.
  • Exams are paper-based and individual, so conceptual mastery remains the core accountability mechanism.
  • The course project is a capstone for applied evidence of professional skill, including responsible AI use.

2) Where AI Use Is Allowed

Homework

  • AI use is allowed for homework.
  • Homework is intentionally lower-stakes practice.
  • Students should use homework to build understanding for exams.

Course Project

  • AI use is allowed and encouraged for project planning, coding support, debugging, and documentation.
  • Students must be able to explain architecture, implementation choices, tradeoffs, and defects.
  • AI-assisted work does not reduce quality expectations.

Studying and Self-Teaching

  • Students are encouraged to use AI for concept review, practice generation, and explaining errors.
  • Students should verify AI outputs with course materials, tests, and runtime behavior.

3) Where AI Use Is Not Allowed

Exams

  • AI tools are not allowed during paper exams.
  • Exams measure each student's personal understanding.

Misrepresentation

  • Submitting AI-generated content as if fully self-authored without disclosure is not acceptable.
  • Copying code or text without understanding it is considered a learning integrity issue.

4) Required AI Transparency

Students must maintain a lightweight AI usage log for major assignments and the project.

Include:

  • Date and task
  • Tool used
  • Prompt summary
  • Output summary
  • What was accepted, modified, or rejected
  • One short reflection on what was learned

A short weekly summary is acceptable when activity is minor.

5) Quackthrough Protocol (AI Rubber Duck Debugging)

A quackthrough is a structured debugging conversation with AI before asking for instructor help.

Students should perform this sequence:

  1. State the expected behavior and actual behavior.
  2. Share the smallest reproducible code snippet.
  3. Ask AI for hypotheses, not only fixes.
  4. Run one change at a time and record outcomes.
  5. Summarize what was learned and what remains blocked.

Students should bring this summary when requesting help.

6) Office Hours Escalation Model

Office hours use AI as first-level supplemental instruction with instructor guidance.

Support flow:

  1. Student attempts a quackthrough.
  2. Student shares log and blocker.
  3. Instructor helps evaluate AI suggestions, correct misconceptions, and decide next steps.
  4. Student implements and verifies with tests or reproducible checks.

This model emphasizes coaching, critical evaluation, and domain judgment over answer-seeking.

7) Responsible Use Standards

Students are expected to:

  • Validate AI output with tests, tracing, and documentation.
  • Protect secrets, credentials, and sensitive data.
  • Avoid pasting restricted or private data into external tools.
  • Cite external sources when AI output includes borrowed material.
  • Ask for conceptual explanations when code is unclear.

8) Instructor Evaluation Lens

Work is evaluated on:

  • Correctness and reliability
  • Conceptual understanding
  • Ability to explain and defend decisions
  • Quality of testing and verification
  • Professional communication of AI-assisted workflow

AI assistance can improve process quality, but it cannot replace demonstrated understanding.

9) Optional Language for Syllabus

Suggested statement:

AI tools are permitted in homework and project work in this course. You are accountable for the correctness of your work and your understanding of all submitted material. Paper exams remain individual and no-AI. Use AI as a learning accelerator, not a substitute for reasoning.
